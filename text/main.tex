\title{Where does the infill housing go?\\Addition versus division}
\author{Thomas Davidoff, Robert Helsley, and Tsur Somerville\thanks{thomas.davidoff@sauder.ubc.ca; robert.helsley@sauder.ubc.ca; tsur.somerville@sauder.ubc.ca}\\Sauder School of Business, University of British Columbia\\\textbf{Under continual revision.} Updated version is available at:\\ https://github.com/tomdavidoff/infillPuzzle/blob/main/text/main.pdf}

\onehalfspacing
\begin{abstract}
	
Jurisdictions across Canada and the U.S. have recently allowed ``missing-middle'' infill housing, such as accessory dwelling units and multiplexes, in residential zones formerly restricted to single-family use. Existing studies document that infill housing is less prevalent in more expensive neighbourhoods within metro areas. This is puzzling because the right to add space should be more valuable in neighbourhoods where land prices are higher. To resolve this contradiction, we develop a parsimonious model that distinguishes between two components of missing-middle reform: ``addition'' (adding to allowable square footage) and ``division'' (permitting the subdivision of a fixed amount of square footage into multiple units).  In the model, addition is more valuable where price per square foot of built space is greatest, while division is more valuable where the elasticity of price in square footage is less positive. With fixed home sizes, wealthy households sort into better locations. If they also have a greater willingness to pay for space, then infill housing will be less prevalent in pricier neighbourhoods when the upzoning features relatively little addition and more division. We test these predictions with housing transactions and permitting data with respect to a division-only 2018 upzoning in Vancouver that allowed duplexes to replace ``single-family'' homes, but with no additional density allowed. Duplex takeup was concentrated in relatively low-price-per-square-foot neighbourhoods, which feature lower elasticity of willingness to pay for space. There is some evidence from Vancouver, Portland, and Minneapolis that policies that mix addition-and-division show less negative correlation between infill takeup and land price.
\par\bigskip
\textbf{Keywords:} housing supply and markets, government policy, regulation, state and local government
\end{abstract}

\section{\label{sec:intro}Introduction}

As concerns over housing affordability have grown in political salience over the last decade, a growing number of U.S. and Canadian jurisdictions have introduced reforms to allow ``missing middle'', ``infill'', or ``gentle density'' housing types in formerly single-family-only residential zones. This housing might take the form of additional density that does not dramatically change the urban fabric such as accessory dwelling units (Vancouver's laneway homes) or ``multiplex'' structures with a range of two to six homes on single lot, or more noticeable changes in urban form in the missing-middle's densest form such as row and townhouses or three- to four-storey apartment buildings. As has been emphasized, e.g. by \textcite{bruecknerSingh} and \textcite{HsiehMoretti}, restrictive zoning causes deadweight loss most where real estate is most valuable. It is thus natural to expect that the option to densify urban land would be exercised most commonly in the neighborhoods where land and structure prices per unit are highest.

The puzzle with missing middle uptake is that several recent empirical studies (cited below) have documented that infill housing is, in fact, less prevalent in highly expensive neighbourhoods. We present a parsimonious model of density choice that shows how where redevelopment occurs can depend on the extent to which policy changes allow the addition of greater density on a lot (``addition'') and the allowance for more units within that density (``division''). These two effects likely work in opposite directions with respect to the relationship between infill prevalence and neighbourhood price levels. In the model, the extent that infill policies allow more square feet to be built on the same lot (addition) lead to greater take-up where the price per square foot of structure net of construction costs is greatest. For a given allowed density, allowing more (i.e. smaller) units to be created out of the same square footage (division) will result in less take-up if wealthier households with a greater elasticity of willingness to pay for square footage sort into more expensive single-family neighbourhoods. That positive sorting is theoretically likely: in standard monocentric models affluent households may suburbanize due to flat bid-rent curves generated by income-elastic demand for housing,\footnote{See \textcite{Wheaton77} and earlier work of Muth, Alonso, Mills, and Beckmann cited therein.} but with single-family zoning leading many households to a fixed (over-) consumption of lot size, the normality of demand for location quality should generate positive sorting.

This paper studies patterns of redevelopment from two very modest, but broad upzonings in Vancouver, BC, in Canada. These upzonings represent different mixes of addition and division. In the first case, allowing duplexes (division) without an increase in allowable floor area (addition). The second policy change allowing up to 8 units on single lot included addition.\footnote{We focus on the reforms that allowed for additional ownership units rather than those that allowed for accessory dwelling units, which are rental only.} We evaluate the spatial correlation between neighbourhood price levels and the prevalence of infill housing permits following the different upzoning. The first policy allowing duplexes without additional floor area has been taken up predominantly in less expensive East Side neighbourhoods. Relatively recently-allowed multiplexes, that allow a bit more density have also been concentrated on the East Side; a quantitative exercise does not make a clear prediction of the siting given the offsetting addition and division effects. (Our empirical evidence here is on the first reform, analysis of the multiplex reform and similar reforms in Portland and Minneapolis are work in progress).

The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. In Section \ref{sec:lit} we review the literature on upzoning. Section \ref{sec:model} presents our model. We follow this with a description of the institutional and policy setting in Section \ref{sec:policies}. Our empirical analysis is in Section \ref{sec:results}. Section \ref{sec:conclusion} concludes. The theoretical model consider small changes to a single type of housing in an open city with two income groups. With fixed home sizes, under minimal assumptions, households are sorted by income, with higher income households in better locations. An increase in allowable space, holding the number of units constant, makes the high-income area expand because of the high-income households' greater willingness to pay for space. An increase in the number of units allowed, holding the total space constant, makes the low-income area expand, again because of the high-income households' greater willingness to pay for space. The model thus predicts by analogy that reforms allowing more space per unit will be taken up predominantly in high-income neighbourhoods, while reforms allowing more units per lot holding space constant will be taken up predominantly in low-income neighbourhoods. The spatial sorting of redevelopment options that mix addition and division (such as Vancouver's multiplex reform of 2023) is ambiguous. The model predicts ``by analogy'' because we cannot easily predict whether sorting will ``flip'' in the presence of a division reform: it is possible that a large reduction in space per unit would trigger an equilibrium in which poorer households live in smaller units closer to downtown, as is possible in the standard monocentric model. 

\section{\label{sec:lit}Related Literature}

This paper draws from several different threads of urban economics. The first is an older literature on urban growth under a monocentric model. The second is the literature on residential sorting, and the third is more current work on the effects of land use regulation reforms. Our contribution is to  examine the sensitivity of residential redevelopment to the form of zoning in the presence of households sorting across space. 

Redevelopment more broadly has been long studied in the literature, beginning with the dynamic models of urban growth such as \textcite{Brueckner_1981}, \textcite{HochmanPines_1980}, and \textcite{Wheaton_1982}. With durable but replaceable capital in a monocentric model and no other neighborhood specific amenities, \textcite{Wheaton_1982} finds that redevelopment occurs with substantial increases in the capital-to-land ratio (density and or structure quality) and that, under standard conditions occurs primarily in the urban core. Those papers have no redevelopment frictions caused by local land use policies. Introducing the restrictions as frictions is important as they have been identified by economists as the primary cause of limits on housing supply that increase housing prices (see \textcite{GyourkoMolloy2015} and \textcite{Molloy2020} for comprehensive reviews of this literature).

Over the last decade there have been broad rezonings enacted in cities around the world. The empirical study of the effects of these large scale upzonings on new supply finds support for the positive effect of relaxing density restrictions on redevelopment at higher densities. 

\textcite{Anagol_etal2021} analyze a broad-based 2016 zoning reform in S\~{a}o Paulo, Brazil, that allowed an average increase in the maximum allowed built area ratio (BAR) - analogous to floor area ratio (FAR) or floor space ratio (FSR) - from 1.54 to 2.09. Over six years they note a 66 percent increase in multi-family permit applications for treated blocks compared to the controls (or .012 permits per block per year) resulting in an aggregate 1.9 percent increase in the stock of housing and a 0.5 percent decline in prices.

Like S\~{a}o Paulo, Auckland, NZ also introduced a single broad upzoning. Implemented by the Auckland Council (the government body administering the metro area) in late 2016, the Auckland Unitary Plan (AUP), increased the allowed number of units and density on about 75 percent of the metro area's residential land (about 90 percent of the upzoned area allowed for three units per parcel, with the remaining 10 percent at higher densities). \textcite{GreenawayPhillips2023} identify a large increase in new construction (equal to an estimated 4.1 percent of the existing housing stock over five years) attributable to the policy changes in the AUP. 

Both \textcite{Anagol_etal2021} and \textcite{GreenawayPhillips2023} study relatively short-run effects, within a five-year period following the rezoning. \textcite{BuchlerLutz2024} are able to examine longer-term effects studying Swiss zoning reforms that increased densities over a multi-decade period starting in the 1990s. They study rolling increases in the allowed maximum density (with a median 30\% increase in density) across the Canton of Zurich from 1995 to 2020 using an event study approach for each hectare in Zurich. For a five-ten year post-reform period they find larger effects than in \textcite{Anagol_etal2021}: a 9 percent increase in living space and housing units in up-zoned compared to not up-zoned areas. As well, uptake is greater where existing buildings are already at the maximum allowed density, i.e. zoning is binding. 

While not the entire city, New York, NY introduced a series of neighbourhood-level rezonings affecting approximately 40 percent of the city between 2004 and 2013 (\textcite{Liao2023}). Using a border discontinuity design for local increases in FAR, \textcite{Liao2023} finds an overall 4 percent increase in housing supply over seven years, and 8 percent for areas that were treated with the largest increases in FAR. Using the same zoning reform, \textcite{Peng2023} creates a spatial model with worker residential mobility and finds as a counterfactual to the observed outcomes a lower effect of a 0.7 percent long-run increase in the stock. 

\textcite{Rollet_2025} follows more explicitly in the spirit of models of redevelopment in including patterns of existing development in the assessment of uptake in the upzoning reforms. He creates a dynamic model of the redevelopment choices made by developers within a dynamic spatial equilibrium for households and firms. He finds that the large fixed costs of redevelopment, especially in dense areas, limits the short-run effects of rezoning. In simulations, after 40 years only 18\% of the allowed additional floor space would actually be added.  

In Minneapolis and Portland that enacted similar zoning changes to Vancouver, with the same greater emphasis on division than addition, studies find mixed effects on supply. \textcite{Dong2024a} assesses the Portland, OR upzoning. He finds that as a result of these changes, ``moderate middle'' housing increased from 13.4 percent to 44.7 percent of new building permits in single family zones, but does not provide an estimate of the total change in housing.\textcite{GuMunro_2025} use synthetic controls to test the effects of the Minnesota reforms, finding lower prices and rents, but surprisingly no change in permits.

Our work looks at how sorting can affect rezoning uptake. Starting with \textcite{Tiebout1956} and with the empirical foundation for effects on house prices in \textcite{Rosen1974}, numerous papers have examined sorting by households across space. Sorting on the basis of amenities has been general (\textcite{Bayer_etal2004}), but often in a US context focused on the sorting effects of preferences for school quality. More recent work, as in \textcite{Couture_etal2024} and \textcite{AlmagoDomingues2025}, has extended this to endogenous amenities, such as gentrification. Our model, like that in \textcite{BayerMcMillan2012} focuses on the joint preference for amenities and house type, though in a more focused framing that identifies the effects of the levers of zoning on sorting. 

Several authors have identified geographic patterns of redevelopment activity following zoning reforms. \textcite{MarantzElmendorfKim2023_ADUReforms} find that accessory dwelling unit (ADU) placement in California is widespread, but concentrated in convenient, low- and moderate-rent neighbourhoods, and not in more expensive neighbourhoods. Similar findings are in \textcite{BruecknerThomaz2024}, who find that ADU permitting rates are lower in higher-priced neighbourhoods, even after controlling for income and other factors. Huang et al (2023) analyze the construction of duplexes and triplexes in Seattle following a 2019 upzoning reform and find that these housing types are more likely to be built in lower-priced neighbourhoods. Finally, \textcite{DongHansz_2019} examine the impact of Portland's Residential Infill Project (RIP) on housing construction patterns and find that the new zoning has led to more infill development in lower-priced areas. 

While the work cited above examines upzoning in individual cities, \textcite{Stacy_etal2023} create a cross-city panel to study the effects of relaxing zoning restrictions on new construction more broadly. They conduct newspaper text searches for approximately 180 land use regulatory changes between 2000 and 2019 in eight US metro areas. About half (98) of these lessen restrictions through either allowing ADUs (35) or increasing FARs (15). If they observe a relaxation in any measure, they observe the discrete characteristic of having loosened restrictions is associated with a 0.8 percent increase in residential postal addresses in the jurisdictions that loosen restrictions, when compared to jurisdictions that do not. In terms of geographic patterns, they find the effects come mainly from above-median rent areas.

We study zoning that allows some combination of increased units or allowed floor area on a given lot. With undeveloped lots, an alternative to allowing duplexes would be halving lot sizes. Using data from Atlanta to calibrate a model, \textcite{Ma_2025} estimates the effect of this type of forced upzoning. In her data, the binding constraint is not allowed density, but division, where large lot zoning yields large single family homes in well off areas. In her model she allows for demand heterogeneity in preferences for unit type and locations. Regulatory costs cause developers to build large units on large lots rather than trying to get permission to subdivide and built multiple smaller units. She finds that forcing upzoning results in increased supply as when a single large unit cannot be built, more smaller units are constructed instead. Similarly, \textcite{vonBergmannLauster} find large empirical gains in rare cases of lot-splitting in Vancouver.


\section{\label{sec:model} A model of sorting and density regulations}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Parcels and Location Quality}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

The city consists of a continuum of land parcels, each of unit size. Each parcel has a  location quality $\theta \in [\underline{\theta}, \bar{\theta}]$, reflecting access to natural, social and economic amenities. We treat $\theta$ as exogenous.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{The Regulatory Environment}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

The regulatory authority sets two zoning instruments: 1. The total floor space permitted on a lot $\bar{S}$. Since all lots are of unit size, $\bar{S}$ is also the \emph{floor space ratio}. 2. 
The number of housing units permitted on a lot $N$. $N$ is presumably closely related to population density. Both constraints bind wherever development occurs. 

Floor space per lot is divided equally among the units, so unit size or floor space consumption per household is
\begin{equation}
  s \equiv \frac{\bar{S}}{N}.
  \label{eq:s}
\end{equation}
The case $N=1$ corresponds to single-family housing, with $s = \bar{S}$.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Utility and Its Components}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Household utility takes the form
\begin{equation}
  U\!\bigl(X,\, V(s, N, \theta)\bigr),
  \label{eq:utility}
\end{equation}
where $X$ is numeraire consumption and $V(s, N, \theta)$ is housing services. We assume that $U$ is increasing and strictly quasi-concave. The partial derivatives of $V$ satisfy:
\[
  V_s > 0, \qquad V_N < 0, \qquad V_\theta > 0.
\]
More floor space and better location quality raise housing services. More units per lot, holding $s$ and $\theta$ fixed, reduce housing services --- a density externality capturing congestion, noise, and loss of privacy from higher density development.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Income Groups and the Open-City Equilibrium}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

There are two household types or income groups in the city, indexed by $i \in \{H, L\}$, with incomes $Y_H > Y_L$. Each household faces the budget constraint $X + P \cdot s = Y_i$, where $P$ is the price per square foot of housing. The city is "open" in the sense that migration to other regions or housing markets is costless, and the income groups have exogenous outside-option utilities $\bar{U}_H > \bar{U}_L$. These represent the utilities that are available to each type elsewhere in the economy. Since migration is costless, each type must receive its outside-option utility level in equilibrium:
\begin{equation}
  U\!\Bigl(Y_i - P_i \cdot s,\; V(s, N, \theta)\Bigr) = \bar{U}_i.
  \label{eq:opencity}
\end{equation}
\eqref{eq:opencity} defines an equilibrium price per square foot of housing such that residents with the same income are indifferent among locations.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Equilibrium Prices and Comparative Statics}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

%------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{The Bid Price Function}
%------------------------------------------------------------------

To characterize this equilibrium price, it will be convenient to let $X^*(\bar{U}_i, V)$ represent the minimum level of numeraire consumption needed to reach $\bar{U}_i$. Its partial derivatives are:

\begin{equation}
  X^*_V \equiv \frac{\partial X^*}{\partial V} = -\frac{U_V}{U_X} < 0, \qquad
  X^*_{\bar{U}} \equiv \frac{\partial X^*}{\partial \bar{U}_i} = \frac{1}{U_X} > 0.
  \label{eq:Xstar}
\end{equation}

The equilibrium bid price per square foot can then be written as
\begin{equation}
  P_i(\theta; \bar{S}, N) = \frac{N}{\bar{S}}\Bigl[Y_i - X^*\!\Bigl(\bar{U}_i,\, V\!\Bigl(\frac{\bar{S}}{N}, N, \theta\Bigr)\Bigr)\Bigr].
  \label{eq:price}
\end{equation}
The term in square brackets is the household's maximum willingness to pay for housing: income minus the minimum level of numeraire consumption needed to reach the outside option. Dividing by $s = \bar{S}/N$ converts this to a per-square-foot price.
%------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Comparative Statics of the Bid Price}
%------------------------------------------------------------------

Differentiating \eqref{eq:price} with respect to $\theta$ yields the slope of the bid price in location quality:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial P_i}{\partial\theta} = \frac{U_V \cdot V_\theta}{U_X \cdot s} = -\frac{X^*_V \cdot V_\theta}{s} > 0.
  \label{eq:dPdtheta}
\end{equation}
Not surprisingly, households bid more for more desirable locations.

Differentiating \eqref{eq:price} with respect to $\bar{S}$ (holding $N$ fixed) and $N$ (holding $\bar{S}$ fixed) gives the impacts of changes in the regulatory instruments:
\begin{align}
  \frac{\partial P_i}{\partial \bar{S}}\bigg|_N &= -\frac{P_i}{\bar{S}} - \frac{X^*_V \cdot V_s}{\bar{S}},
  \label{eq:dPdSbar}\\[6pt]
  \frac{\partial P_i}{\partial N}\bigg|_{\bar{S}} &= \frac{P_i}{N} + X^*_V\!\left(-\frac{V_s}{N} + \frac{N \cdot V_N}{\bar{S}}\right).
  \label{eq:dPdN}
\end{align}

In \eqref{eq:dPdSbar}, increasing $\bar{S}$ raises $s$, which raises housing services through $V_s > 0$, reducing $X^*$ and increasing total willingness to pay ($-X^*_V V_s/\bar{S} > 0$); but it also spreads that willingness to pay over more square feet, reducing the per-square-foot price ($-P_i/\bar{S} < 0$). The net effect is ambiguous. Conversely, in \eqref{eq:dPdN}, increasing $N$ reduces $s$, concentrating willingness to pay over fewer square feet and raising the per-square-foot price ($P_i/N > 0$); but smaller units lower housing services through $V_s$, raising $X^*$ and reducing total willingness to pay ($X^*_V V_s/N < 0$ since $X^*_V < 0$), and the density externality further reduces housing services through $V_N < 0$ ($N X^*_V V_N/\bar{S} < 0$). The net effect is again ambiguous.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{The Slope Condition and Single-Crossing}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Differentiating \eqref{eq:dPdtheta} with respect to $Y_i$ gives:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial^2 P_i}{\partial Y_i\,\partial\theta}
  = -\frac{V_\theta}{s}\cdot\frac{\partial X^*_V}{\partial X}
  = \frac{V_\theta}{s}\cdot\frac{U_{VX}\,U_X - U_V\,U_{XX}}{U_X^2}.
  \label{eq:crosspd}
\end{equation}
Given our earlier assumptions about $U(X,V)$, a sufficient condition for $\frac{\partial^2 P_i}{\partial Y_i\,\partial\theta} > 0$  is  $U_{VX} \geq 0$. This also implies that the marginal value of housing services is non-decreasing in income. In what follows, we will assume that this condition holds.

This implies that $P_H(\theta)$ is steeper than $P_L(\theta)$ for all $\theta \in [\underline{\theta}, \bar{\theta}]$, so that the bid price schedules can cross at most once. To ensure that they do cross we assume that the incomes and outside-option utilities of the two groups are such that $P_H(\bar{\theta}) > P_L(\bar{\theta})$ and $P_L(\underline{\theta}) > P_H(\underline{\theta})$. Then there will be a unique $\theta^* \in (\underline\theta, \bar\theta)$, the sorting threshold, such that $P_H(\theta^*) = P_L(\theta^*)$.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{The Sorting Threshold $\theta^*$}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

%------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Definition of Income Sorting}
%------------------------------------------------------------------

The sorting threshold $\theta^*$ partitions locations by the income of residents: low-income households occupy parcels with $\theta < \theta^*$ and high-income households occupy parcels with $\theta > \theta^*$. The equilibrium price per square foot is the upper envelope of the bid prices of the two groups:  $P^*(\theta) = \max\{P_H(\theta), P_L(\theta)\}$.

%------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Comparative Statics of $\theta^*$}
%------------------------------------------------------------------

From \eqref{eq:price}, the condition $P_H(\theta^*) = P_L(\theta^*)$ can be written as:
\begin{equation}
  Y_H - Y_L = X^*\!\Bigl(\bar{U}_H, V\!\Bigl(\tfrac{\bar{S}}{N}, N, \theta^*\Bigr)\Bigr) - X^*\!\Bigl(\bar{U}_L, V\!\Bigl(\tfrac{\bar{S}}{N}, N, \theta^*\Bigr)\Bigr).
  \label{eq:tstar_implicit}
\end{equation}
Then the implicit function theorem gives, after a bit of algebra,
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial\theta^*}{\partial\bar{S}}\bigg|_N = -\frac{V_s}{N\,V_\theta} < 0,
  \label{eq:dts_dSbar}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial\theta^*}{\partial N}\bigg|_{\bar{S}} = \frac{\frac{\bar{S}}{N^2}V_s - V_N}{V_\theta} > 0.
  \label{eq:dts_dN}
\end{equation}

In contrast to the comparative statics of the bid price in \eqref{eq:dPdSbar} and \eqref{eq:dPdN}, the effects of the regulatory instruments on $\theta^*$ are unambiguous. Increasing structural density $\bar{S}$ (holding $N$ fixed) increases floor space per unit $s = \bar{S}/N$, which increases the level of housing services. Since the marginal value of housing services is higher for high-income households, they outbid low-income households in a neighborhood of the sorting threshold and $\theta^*$ falls. Increasing unit density $N$ (holding $\bar{S}$ fixed) reduces housing services by reducing space consumption and exacerbating the density externality. Both effects reduce housing services disproportionately for high-income households, pushing $\theta^*$ upward.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Costs, Profit, and the Zero-Profit Condition}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

The profit from developing a parcel of quality $\theta$ is
\begin{equation}
  \pi(\theta) = P^*(\theta)\cdot\bar{S} - C(\bar{S}, N),
  \label{eq:profit}
\end{equation}
where $C(\bar{S}, N)$ is the cost of building $\bar{S}$ square feet of space divided into $N$ equally sized units. We assume construction cost is increasing and convex in both arguments. Under free entry, developers build wherever $\pi(\theta) \geq 0$, that is, where $P^*(\theta) \geq C(\bar{S}, N)/\bar{S}$, the construction cost per square foot. Since $P^*(\theta)$ is increasing in $\theta$, there is a unique lowest quality parcel where development occurs. This development margin $\hat\theta$ is defined by:
\begin{equation}
  P^*(\hat\theta) = \frac{C(\bar{S}, N)}{\bar{S}}.
  \label{eq:thetahat}
\end{equation}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{The Development Margin $\hat\theta$ and Equilibrium Land Allocation}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

%------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Equilibrium Development Pattern}
%------------------------------------------------------------------

The equilibrium development pattern depends on the position of $\hat\theta$ relative to $\theta^*$. Assuming that some but not all land is developed leaves two cases:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{High-income development} ($\hat\theta \geq \theta^*$): construction costs are high enough that only parcels attractive to high-income households are profitable. The development pattern is:
  \[
    \begin{cases} \text{Undeveloped} & \theta < \hat\theta \\ \text{High-income housing} & \theta \in [\hat\theta, \bar\theta] \end{cases}
  \]
  \item \textbf{Mixed development} ($\hat\theta < \theta^*$): the development margin lies below the sorting threshold. The binding bid at $\hat\theta$ is $P_L$, so $P_L(\hat\theta) = C(\bar{S},N)/\bar{S}$, and the development pattern is:
  \[
    \begin{cases} \text{Undeveloped} & \theta < \hat\theta \\ \text{Low-income housing} & \theta \in [\hat\theta, \theta^*) \\ \text{High-income housing} & \theta \in [\theta^*, \bar\theta] \end{cases}
  \]
\end{enumerate}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{The Comparative Statics of $\hat\theta$}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

For the mixed development case, the development margin $\hat\theta$ satisfies $P_L(\hat\theta)\cdot\bar{S} - C(\bar{S},N) = 0$. Applying the implicit function theorem to this expression gives, after a bit of algebra,
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial\hat\theta}{\partial\bar{S}}\bigg|_N
  = -\frac{\bar{S}\,\frac{\partial P_L}{\partial\bar{S}}\big|_N + P_L - C_{\bar{S}}}{\bar{S}\cdot\partial P_L/\partial\theta}
  = -\frac{-X^*_V V_s - C_{\bar{S}}}{\bar{S}\cdot\partial P_L/\partial\theta},
  \label{eq:dthat_dSbar}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial\hat\theta}{\partial N}\bigg|_{\bar{S}}
  = -\frac{\bar{S}\,\frac{\partial P_L}{\partial N}\big|_{\bar{S}} - C_N}{\bar{S}\cdot\partial P_L/\partial\theta}
  = -\frac{\frac{\bar{S}}{N}P_L - \frac{\bar{S}}{N}X^*_V V_s + NX^*_V V_N - C_N}{\bar{S}\cdot\partial P_L/\partial\theta},
  \label{eq:dthat_dN}
\end{equation}

where the second expression in \eqref{eq:dthat_dSbar} uses \eqref{eq:dPdSbar} and the second expression in \eqref{eq:dthat_dN} uses \eqref{eq:dPdN}. The numerators are $\partial\pi/\partial\bar{S}|_N$ and $\partial\pi/\partial N|_{\bar{S}}$ respectively, and since the denominator $(\partial P_L/\partial\theta)\cdot\bar{S} > 0$, the sign of each comparative static equals the sign of the corresponding profit derivative: a regulatory change that raises profit lowers $\hat\theta$ and expands development.

The sign of each profit derivative depends in turn on whether the regulated value of the instrument lies below or above its profit-maximising level. Let $(\bar{S}^*, N^*)$ denote the unconstrained optimum, where both profit derivatives are zero. Since profit is concave in each instrument, $\partial\pi/\partial\bar{S}|_N \gtrless 0$ as $\bar{S} \lessgtr \bar{S}^*$, and $\partial\pi/\partial N|_{\bar{S}} \gtrless 0$ as $N \lessgtr N^*$. Therefore:
\begin{itemize}
  \item If $\bar{S} < \bar{S}^*$: profit is increasing in $\bar{S}$, so raising $\bar{S}$ lowers $\hat\theta$ and expands development. If $\bar{S} > \bar{S}^*$: profit is falling, so raising $\bar{S}$ raises $\hat\theta$ and contracts development.
  \item If $N < N^*$: profit is increasing in $N$, so raising $N$ lowers $\hat\theta$ and expands development. If $N > N^*$: profit is falling, so raising $N$ raises $\hat\theta$ and contracts development.
\end{itemize}
In both cases, regulation that holds an instrument below its profit-maximising level is development-suppressing. The empirically natural case --- the constrained regime where zoning sets binding maxima below the profit-maximising levels --- gives $\partial\hat\theta/\partial\bar{S}|_N < 0$ and $\partial\hat\theta/\partial N|_{\bar{S}} < 0$: relaxing either restriction expands the developed area and the range of location qualities that can be developed for low-income housing. Notably, regulation can also be development-suppressing when it mandates values \emph{above} the profit-maximising levels --- buildings that are too large or too dense reduce profit and raise $\hat\theta$ just as surely as restrictions that are too tight.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Interpretation: Regulation and the Allocation of Land}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

The range of location qualities available to low-income households is $[\hat\theta, \theta^*)$, which expands when $\hat\theta$ falls, $\theta^*$ rises, or both. Since $s = \bar{S}/N$, changes in the two policy instruments affect floor space per unit according to:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{ds}{s} = \frac{d\bar{S}}{\bar{S}} - \frac{dN}{N}.
  \label{eq:ds_pct}
\end{equation}
Floor space per unit rises, stays constant, or falls according to whether the percentage change in $\bar{S}$ exceeds, equals, or falls short of the percentage change in $N$. The effects on both thresholds can be organised around this decomposition. We consider five cases, working in the constrained regime ($\bar{S} < \bar{S}^*$, $N < N^*$) for $\hat\theta$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Case 1: Only $\bar{S}$ rises ($dN = 0$, $ds > 0$).} Larger units raise housing services. High-income households benefit more at the margin, so they outbid low-income households in a wider range of locations and $\theta^*$ falls unambiguously. In the constrained regime, larger floor space raises $P_L$ and profit, so $\hat\theta$ falls unambiguously. Both thresholds fall, so the effect on the low-income range $[\hat\theta, \theta^*)$ depends on which falls by more and is therefore ambiguous.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Case 2: $\bar{S}$ grows faster than $N$ ($d\bar{S}/\bar{S} > dN/N > 0$, $ds > 0$).} As in Case 1, $s$ rises, but now the density externality also worsens. The fall in $\theta^*$ from the larger units is offset to some degree by the rise from the density externality, leaving the net effect on $\theta^*$ ambiguous. In the constrained regime $\hat\theta$ falls unambiguously. The low-income range may expand from below but the upper boundary is uncertain.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Case 3: $\bar{S}$ and $N$ grow proportionally ($d\bar{S}/\bar{S} = dN/N > 0$, $ds = 0$).} Floor space per unit is unchanged, so only the density externality operates. More units worsen congestion, reducing housing services disproportionately for high-income households and raising $\theta^*$ unambiguously. In the constrained regime, revenue rises faster than costs so profit rises, lowering $\hat\theta$ unambiguously. The range $[\hat\theta, \theta^*)$ expands at both ends. This is the unique configuration with $d\bar{S}, dN > 0$ that unambiguously improves low-income housing supply.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Case 4: $N$ grows faster than $\bar{S}$ ($dN/N > d\bar{S}/\bar{S} > 0$, $ds < 0$).} Smaller units and the density externality both reduce housing services for high-income households, raising $\theta^*$ unambiguously. The effect on $\hat\theta$ is ambiguous: smaller units reduce $P_L$ and profit (raising $\hat\theta$), while more units in the constrained regime raise profit (lowering $\hat\theta$). The low-income range expands from above but not necessarily from below.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Case 5: Only $N$ rises ($d\bar{S} = 0$, $ds < 0$).} Smaller units reduce housing services and the density externality worsens. Both push $\theta^*$ upward unambiguously. In the constrained regime $\hat\theta$ falls unambiguously. The low-income range expands from above and from below.

\subsection{\label{sec:policies} Policy Changes}

\subsubsection{City of Vancouver Duplex Reform}

In September, 2018, the City of Vancouver allowed homes in zones that previously allowed only a single family residence (possibly with a basement suite and a laneway home, both rental-only) to be built in the form of two (half) duplex units (each duplex with a rental basement suite, but no laneway home).\footnote{See \texttt{https://council.vancouver.ca/20181218/documents/a5.pdf}.} This reform was pure division and no addition, in that the ratio of floor space to lot size (``FSR'') was not increased for duplex units. Empirically, B.C. Assessment inventory data, shows only a very small difference in FSR between single family and duplexes built at that time. Among single family and duplex homes built on standard 33' lots with effective years between 2019 and 2023 (the date of a subsequent reform), duplex units have a mean and median FSR of .37 (so .74 for the pair), whereas single family home main buildings have mean and medians of .69 and .71. This change applied to most ``RS'' zones, which became uniformly labelled ``R1-1'' or ``R1'' in a 2023 reform.

We expect duplex homes to replace existing single-family homes in locations where duplex homes sell for more per square foot relative to single family homes than in other locations. Our model predicts under plausible conditions, that this price wedge would be greatest where price per square foot of single family homes is lowest. We do not have a clear prediction on whether the choice to tear down a home would be likelier in areas with higher or lower price per square foot. Generally, we might expect depreciation and the gains to redevelopment to be greater in more expensive neighbourhoods.\footnote{A second-order prediction is that there might be a relative acceleration in tear-downs in areas where the duplex option has more value around the time of the reform. This is challenging to verify in our data, which has only a short sample prior to the reform, and which features a general trend of relatively increasing prices in less expensive locations, as well as a dynamic policy environment with changes such as foreign buyer and empty homes taxes being enacted around the time of the zoning reform.}

\subsubsection{City of Vancouver Multiplex Reform}

In advance of changes initiated by the Province of British Columbia to eliminate all single family zoning and allow a minimum of three units in all single family zones, the City of Vancouver extended multiplex permissions to all formerly single family and low density townhomes residential zones.\footnote{See \texttt{https://council.vancouver.ca/20230914/documents/phea1sr.pdf}.} The guidelines allowed a maximum of four units on lots with frontage from 10.0m (32.8 ft) and less than 13.4m (44'); up to five units for lots with a frontage from 13.4m (44') to less than 15.1m (49.5'); and up to six units *(eight units if all are rental) with frontage of 15.1m (49.5') and higher. A standard 33’ × 122’ lot would allow three units and the less common 50' x 122' would allow up to six units.\footnote{There is an additional minimum site depth requirement of 30.4m (99.7 ft) and a minimum site area of 280m\textsuperscript{2} (3,013.9 sq ft) to allow for more than three units. There are no parking requirements}. 

With the 2023 changes, maximum allowed FAR depended on several factors. Single-family homes and duplexes were permitted maximum FSRs of 0.6 and 0.70, respectively. For multiplexes (three or more units), the default maximum was 0.7, with 1.0 FSR attainable if one of the following applied: (i) all units are secured-rental housing; (ii) one unit is designated as ``below-market'' homeownership; or (iii) the developer pays a density bonus, which can vary from \$3.00 to \$140.00 per square foot of additional density depending on lot size and location. The cost of the density of this bonusing appears to be lower than land prices per buildable square foot in all locations. Including the multiplex policy in our analysis will depend on having a few more periods of data as permit applications only started near the end of our data analysis window.

The next empirical question is whether location quality and elasticity of willingness to pay per square foot are empirically correlated in the City of Vancouver. Figure \ref{fig:diffDuplex} plots the spatially-weighted average log transaction price difference between duplex homes and single family homes between 2014 and 2018 for each permit location 2019-2023. To test empirically whether in the case of division alone, the incidence of redevelopment falls with increased amenity levels or the returns to addition (greater floor area) we assess the determinants of whether an existing older single family home is redeveloped as a new single family unit or as two duplex units. Table \ref{tab:horseRace} presents lot-level logit regressions of the choice to build a duplex (1) or a single family home (0) on redeveloped lots. In all regressions we include lot size (permitLogArea) as a control. Our direct measure of amenities (proxy for $\theta$ in the model) is the redeveloped property's BCA estimated land value per square foot (logBcaLandPPSF), from the lot's July 2018 BCA assessed values (hence pre-upzoning). The most salient measure of willingness to pay for incremental floor space (expected to covary negatively with $\theta$) is the price per square foot difference between single family homes and duplexes (larger value, more relative valuation of single family homes, elasticity\_duplexPenalty33, estimated only on 33' lots). An alternative measure presenting similar but unreported results is the locally weighted elasticity of transaction price with respect to lot size for single family homes only (elasticity\_singleFamilyPre). The single-duplex price per square foot measure is more direct, but the single family measure may be more precise in that prior to 2019, duplexes were concentrated in a small number of ``RT'' zones, so local price differences reflect a mix of location and housing type. (The single family measure is imperfect, as lot sizes are spatially correlated as seen in Figures \ref{fig:plotDuplexSingle33} and \ref{fig:plotDuplexSingle50}. The results are a horse race between the different ways to address sorting and the likelihood of redevelopment as more smaller units. Across specifications, smaller lots are likelier to be redeveloped as duplexes rather than single-family, consistent with Figure \ref{fig:plotDuplexSingle33} and \ref{fig:plotDuplexSingle50}. Assessed land value per land square foot, the $\theta$ proxy, is strongly negatively associated with duplex choice, consistent with the model. Also consistent with the model, the pre-2018-reform single-duplex price per square foot differential is negatively associated with duplex choice. In specification (3), though, we see that land value per square foot ``wins the horse race'', in the sense that the coefficient on the single-duplex differential falls more in the presence of the $\theta$ control than vice-versa. It is thus not true that the price per square foot negative sorting observed in Vancouver can be explained away by our elasticity measure. In fact, closer to the opposite. Similar results apply to the single-family-based elasticity measure (and a similar measure of strata price per square foot elasticity).

We see two ways to interpret the ``horse race'' result that price per square
foot beats the elasticity measure as an explanation for duplex choice. The
first is that our model is somehow not salient to builder choice. One
possibility would be that single family homes are commonly ``custom'' projects
in which the end-user buys land and pays a contractor to build. Duplexes are
generally ``spec'' projects in which a builder pays for land and construction
and then sells completed homes to the market. Local builders appear not to be
highly concentrated and could be relatively small-time players who are
liquidity constrained and seek to buy inexpensive land for spec projects.

A second possibility is that builders do not distinguish between price level
and elasticity conceptually because the two are so highly correlated.
Elasticity of willingness to pay is worse measured as it is a second-order
versus first-order measure. In addition to the high correlation within
Vancouver mentioned above, we estimate land value correlations and elasticities
of assessed land value with respect to size of .33 within census tracts and .25
within typically somewhat larger neighbourhoods. When observations are weighted
by inverse standard errors of the elasticity estimate, that correlation rises
to .52 and .57 respectively. For jurisdictions, the values are .6 unweighted
and .83 weighted. That the correlations approach one as measurement error for
the elasticity rises suggests a true value sufficiently close to one that
industry participants may conflate the two.

\section{\label{sec:conclusion} Conclusion}

We present a simple model of infill housing benefits that distinguishes between the benefits of additional square footage and the ability to divide square footage. We find elasticities of price with respect to square footage generally significantly below one, consistent with strong demand for the division effect. This model does a fair but incomplete job of explaining the seeming puzzle of stronger uptake of infill where real estate is less valuable, so that the addition effect is weaker.

In standard urban models, there is uncertainty over the correlation between price per square foot and incomes (and hence, presumably, demand for division). This is because both locational quality (e.g. time saved commuting and public goods) and space are normal goods. When zoning binds so households are largely forced to overconsume space, households have limited scope to sort on demand for space, and hence sorting is strongly positive on income across locations.

In Vancouver, there are generally negative correlations between price per square foot and infill activity across different specifications of geography. There is relatively little difference, particularly in recent years in uptake of the laneway home option among single family homes, consistent with offsetting price per square foot effects (larger on the West Side) and privacy costs and small-unit benefit effects (smaller and larger respectively on the East Side). Duplex units involve only a division effect, with no addition benefit (and indeed lost laneway square footage). Consistent with our model, duplex units are roughly twice as popular on the cheaper (relatively!) and less affluent East Side than the West Side. 

Multiplexes have both addition and division effects and their locational sorting is more ambiguous than duplexes.
