\subsection{Policy Changes}

\subsubseciton{City of Vancouver Duplex Reform}

In September, 2018, the City of Vancouver allowed homes in zones that previously allowed only a single family residence (possibly with a basement suite and a laneway home, both rental-only) to be built in the form of two (half) duplex units (each duplex with a rental basement suite, but no laneway home).\footnote{See \texttt{https://council.vancouver.ca/20181218/documents/a5.pdf}.} This reform was pure division and no addition, in that the ratio of floor space to lot size (``FSR'') was not increased for duplex units. Empirically, B.C. Assessment inventory data, shows only a very small difference in FSR between single family and duplexes built at that time. Among single family and duplex homes built on standard 33' lots with effective years between 2019 and 2023 (the date of a subsequent reform), duplex units have a mean and median FSR of .37 (so .74 for the pair), whereas single family home main buildings have mean and medians of .69 and .71. This change applied to most ``RS'' zones, which became uniformly labelled ``R1-1'' or ``R1-1'' in a 2023 reform.

Our model predicts that developers would be most prone to build a duplex rather than a single family home, conditional on tearing down and replacing an existing home in areas where the difference in price between single family and duplex homes is greatest.\footnote{Techincally, this difference has to be adjusted by the higher cost psf of building two smaller units - for instance two kitchens rather than one.} Our model further predicts under plausible conditions, that this price wedge would be greatest where price per square foot of single family homes was lowest. We do not have a clear prediction on whether the choice to tear down a home would be likelier in areas with higher or lower price per square foot. Generally, we might expect depreciation and the gains to redevelopment to be greater in more expensive neighbourhoods.\footnote{A second-order prediction is that there might be a relative acceleration in tear-downs in areas where the duplex option has more value around the time of the reform. This is challenging to verify in our data, which has only a short sample prior to the reform, and which features a general trend of relatively increasing prices in less expensive locations, as well as a dynamic policy environment with changes such as foreign buyer and empty homes taxes being enacted around the time of the zoning reform.}

\subsubsection{City of Vancouver Multiplex Reform}

In advance of changes initiated by the Province of British Columbia to eliminate all single family zoning and allow a minimum of three units in all single family zones, the City of Vancouver extended multiplex permissions to all formerly single family and low density townhomes residential zones.\footnote{See \texttt{https://council.vancouver.ca/20230914/documents/phea1sr.pdf}.} The guidelines allowed a maximum of four units on lots with frontage from 10.0m (32.8 ft) and less than 13.4m (44'); up to five units for lots with a frontage from 13.4m (44') to less than 15.1m (49.5'); and up to six units *(eight units if all are rental) with frontage of 15.1m (49.5') and higher. A standard 33’ × 122’ lot would allow three units and the less common 50' x 122' would allow up to six units.\footnote{There is an additional minimum site depth requirement of 30.4m (99.7 ft) and a minimum site area of 280m\textsuperscript{2} (3,013.9 sq ft) to allow for more than three units. There are no parking requirements}. 

With the 2023 changes, maximum allowed FAR depended on several factors. Single-family homes and duplexes were permitted maximum FSRs of 0.6 and 0.70, respectively. For multiplexes (three or more units), the default maximum was 0.7, with 1.0 FSR attainable if one of the following applied: (i) all units are secured-rental housing; (ii) one unit is designated as ``below-market'' homeownership; or (iii) the developer pays a density bonus, which can vary from \$3.00 to \$140.00 per square foot of additional density depending on lot size and location. The cost of the density of this bonusing appears to be lower than land prices per buildable square foot in all locations. Including the multiplex policy in our analysis will depend on having a few more periods of data as permit applications only started near the end of our data analysis window.

\subsubsection{Analysis}

We identify building permits issued in R1 zones between 2019 and 2023 on parcels that were formerly single-family homes (there were many grandfathered duplexes in R1 zones, with more or less concentration \textit{ex-ante}). The location of duplex and single family permits are plotted in Figure \ref{fig:plotDuplexSingle}. The top panel plots the location of permits on 33-foot-wide lots (the most common width) and the bottom panel on 50' lots (the next most common). Two patterns stand out: at both widths, duplexes are relatively more common on the East Side of Vancouver than the West Side. The West Side, with more green space, better beach access, and proximity to UBC, has higher prices than the East Side. Both sides of the city have similar vehcile access to downtown. 

The second notable pattern is that duplexes are a more common choice on 33' foot lots than 50' lots. This is not easy to explain, in that to the extent single family homes are oversized, they would be more oversized on a 50' lot as the 0.7 FSR allowance (with rental basement suite) is common to lots of all sizes. An explanation put forward by industry participants in casual discussion is that 50' lots are so expensive, that a single duplex unit on a 50' lot can be nearly as expensive as a single family home on a 33' lot in a slightly worse neighbourhood. Developers claim there is thus a very limited clientele that prefers the duplex unit in this case.

Figure \ref{fig:pricePerSqFt} plots the price per square foot of single family homes in 2018, just before the reform, across locations in Vancouver. The pattern is similar to the pattern of duplex choice, with higher prices per square foot on the West Side and lower prices on the East Side. Figure \ref{fig:surface_LocalPPSF} plots for each single family (as of 2018) lot the estimated price per square foot of lot size in two ways. In the top panel, residuals from a full-sample regression of single family transaction prices on lot size, structure year built, and transaction year are plotted (square foot of structure is excluded to avoid separately estimating structure and lot elasticities and because lot size and structure are very highly correlated due to binding FSR constraints for modern-era completions). The land value residuals are distance-weighted for the nearest 250 transactions, with a Gaussian kernel.

In the bottom panel of Figure \ref{fig:pricePerSqFt}, 2019 BC Assessments (based on observations in Summer, 2018, prior to rezoning) estimates of land value per lot square foot are plotted. Greater within-neighborhood variation in the BC Assessments land value estimates may reflect assessors' use of lot-level characteristics, ignored in the spatially weighted average pricing. Both measures show a concentration of value on the West Side, but the East-West difference is greater relative to local differences in the transaction-based panel. The lot-level correlation between the two measures is .8.

Combining Figures \ref{fig:plotDuplexSingle} and \ref{fig:pricePerSqFt}, the pattern of duplex choice across locations in Vancouver is consistent with the model's prediction that with pure division only, infill is more likely to occur in areas where amenity values are lower., i.e. in areas with lower price per square foot.

The next empirical question is whether location quality and elasticity of willingness to pay per square foot are empirically correlated in the City of Vancouver. Figure \ref{fig:diffDuplex} plots the spatially-weighted average log transaction price difference between duplex homes and single family homes between 2014 and 2018 for each permit location. This is the most natural measure of the relevant local elasticity of willingness to pay with respect to size, as duplex homes on the same size lot are typically roughly one-half the size of single family homes. We see that the negative percentage price difference between duplex and single family homes is generally closer to zero on the East Side where duplexes are prevalent. The correlation between the duplex premium and the single family transaction price per square foot measure is a remarkably large -.87. There is a mechanical negative correlation here because single family price levels enter both measures, but the correlation between duplex average price per square foot and the duplex premium (mechanically positive) is -.9. These results suggest that builders may not differentiate analytically between these different measures in their choice between elasticity and price per square foot.

To test empirically whether in the case of division alone, the incidence of redevelopment falls with increased amenity levels or the returns to addition (greater floor area) we assess the determonants of whether an existing older singgle family home is redeveloped as a new single family unit or as two duplex units. Table \ref{tab:horseRace} presents lot-level logit regressions of the choice to build a duplex (1) or a single family home (0) on redeveloped lots. In all regressions we include lot size (permitLogArea) as a control. Our direct measure of amenities is the redeveloped property's BCA estimated land value per square foot (logBcaLandPPSF), from the lot's July 2018 BCA assessed values (hence pre-upzoning). For the marginal willingness to pay for an additional square foot of space in single family use, we estimate a locally weighted elasticity of transaction price with respect to lot size for a single family home (slope_singleFamilyPre). For each redeveloped lot this is estimated with a distance weighted Gaussian kernal applied to single family home transactions from 2014-18. Our alternative is the premium per square foot of single family vs duplex space .\footnote{We estimate this for duplexes that were built prior to the 2018 reform in non-single family zoned areas.} 

The results are a horse race between the different ways to address sorting and the likelihood of redevelopment as more smaller units. Uniformly lots with higher pre-2018 land values per square foot (higher $\theta$) are less likely to be redeveloped as duplexes (columns (1), (4), and (5)). The sign on the estimated coefficient on the willingness to pay for single family space in column (2) is also negative, but it is not statistically different from 0. The redevelopment as duplexes falls as the difference between the marginal willingness to pay for single family space and duplex space rises. The estimated coefficient is statistically different than 0 on its own in column (3) but not if we include land value in column (4). It is not true that including an elasticity measure explains away the correlation between price and duplex choice, in fact the opposite (see column (5)). We see two ways to interpret the ``horse race'' result that price per square
foot beats the elasticity measure as an explanation for duplex choice. The
first is that our model is somehow not salient to builder choice. One possibility would be that single family homes are commonly ``custom'' projects in which the end-user buys land and pays a contractor to build. Duplexes are generally ``spec'' projects in which a builder pays for land and construction and then sells completed homes to the market. Local builders appear not to be highly concentrated and could be relatively small-time players who are liquidity constrained and seek to buy inexpensive land for spec projects.

A second possibility is that builders do not distinguish between price level
and elasticity conceptually because the two are so highly correlated.
Elasitcity of willingness to pay is worse measured as it is a second-order
versus first-order measure. In addition to the high correlation within
Vancouver mentioned above, we estimate land value correlations and elasticities
of assessed land value with respect to size of .33 within census tracts and .25
within typically somewhat larger neighbourhoods. When observations are weighted
by inverse standard errors of the elasticity estimate, that correlation rises
to .52 and .57 respectively. For jurisidctions, the values are .6 unweighted
and .83 weighted. That the correlations approach one as measurement error for
the elasticity rises suggests a true value sufficiently close to one that
industry participants may conflate the two.
